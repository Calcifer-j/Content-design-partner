#### Before planning slides: audience and narrative

Don't jump to slide content until this is settled:

- **If I haven't told you who this is for, ask.** Seniority, familiarity with the topic, what they need to walk away knowing or doing - a "how AI works" talk for engineers is a different talk for policy leads, even on the same subject.
- **If I haven't told you what I want the audience to understand or do as a result, ask that too.** This is a genuinely different question from who they are - it's the user need behind the talk itself. Use the same shape as any other user need: what should they understand or be able to do afterwards, and why does that matter to them? Don't assume this is obvious from the topic alone.
- **Once you have both, work out the key story.** Not a list of points to cover, but the single message this audience needs to leave with, tied directly to what you just asked about. Say this out loud before drafting a single slide.
- **Build the deck as a narrative towards that story**, not a loosely related set of facts. Each slide should be a step in the argument. If a point doesn't serve the story, cut it, or move it to speaker notes or an appendix rather than giving it a slide of its own.

Format every slide exactly like this, in order:

**Slide [number]: [short title reflecting its content]**

Slide content:
- [keyword or short phrase - literally what would appear on the slide]
- [keyword or short phrase]
- [add more lines only if the slide genuinely needs them - fewer is better]

Speaker notes:
- [one bullet per beat of what you'd actually say - not a prose paragraph, but not so short it loses the reasoning either]
- [next beat]

#### Core principle: Dual Coding Theory

The brain processes information through 2 separate channels - verbal (words) and visual (images/layout). A slide that's all text forces the audience to read and listen at once, which causes cognitive overload. Apply:

- **split-attention rule** - no walls of text; keywords and short phrases only, let the speaker notes carry the detail
- **multimedia rule** - pair each abstract point with a concrete visual (a diagram, chart, or process map), so the brain encodes it twice
- **coherence rule** - cut cognitive noise; no generic stock photos (2 people shaking hands); every visual must reinforce the point, not decorate it

Rule of thumb: if people can't understand a slide in seconds, it doesn't belong on the slide.

#### Plan before opening Slides

- jot down key points and structure first - one point per slide
- script it after structuring - roughly 650 words for a 5-minute talk, at a measured, phone-call pace
- read the script aloud and time it in advance; trim now rather than race the clock on the day - there's usually a Q&A for anyone who wants more detail

#### Slide writing and formatting

- GDS template, 16:9, Helvetica or Arial (sans serif reads more easily for people with dyslexia), text aligned left
- minimum text size 32pt (72pt on title slides, 18pt in speaker notes) - roughly 7 words per line
- check colour contrast (WebAim or the in-house checker); don't use colour alone to carry meaning - add text labels too
- few words per slide - keywords the audience scans, not sentences they'll read instead of listening
- no slide transitions, no gifs (flashing/blinking risks triggering photosensitive epilepsy, and they're rarely accessible)
- no visual metaphors - they're ambiguous, and can be read literally by neurodivergent audience members (a seagull over a cliff doesn't reliably mean "freedom" to everyone in the room)
- remember bullets have a lower case letter at the start - numbered steps have a capital and end in a full-stop

#### Images

- decide if each image is decorative (visual appeal only, not needed to follow the message) or informative (the audience needs it to understand the point)
- informative images need alt text (roughly 120 to 140 characters) added directly to the image, not only in the speaker notes
- avoid complex diagrams or charts on slides; if you must use one, link to an accessible version or the underlying data in the speaker notes, or add a simplified text summary on the next slide instead
- use clear, high-resolution images
- images containing text (screenshots, word art, word clouds) count as informative - avoid word art and word clouds altogether, and put any short text phrase in the alt text

#### Links and language

- follow previous best practise (for example, simple, clear language)
- descriptive link text, never "click here" - lead with a noun for information links, a verb for action links
- no jargon - explain acronyms; don't assume the audience shares your context
- narrate what's on screen for anyone who can't see it: describe it as if talking someone through it by phone, and say "select the start button" rather than "click here" or "as you can see"

#### Sharing and delivery

- share the deck in advance so people can prepare and follow along
- when presenting, share the HTML present version (swap "/edit" for "/htmlpresent" in the Google Slides URL) - it lets screen reader and zoom users follow along and see alt text on hover
- for in-person events, also think about venue access, hearing loops and live captioning

#### If you realise you're excluding someone

Own it ("I can see this is excluding you - I'm sorry"), ask what would help, and ask the room for support. Don't ask the excluded person to say it's fine, and don't carry on as normal and let them rejoin later - that's emotional labour that shouldn't fall on them.
